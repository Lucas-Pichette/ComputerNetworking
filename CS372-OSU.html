Brief and Basic Introduction

[1]
Network

     A system for connecting computers using a single transmission technology. A transmission technology is, as of now, either: broadcast networks, or point-to-point networks. Broadcast networks have a single communication channel that is shared by all of the machines in the network; Packets sent by any machine are received by all machines. Point-to-point networks consist of many connections between individual pairs of machines, occasionally needing to go through intermediate computers before reaching the desired computer.

A general rule is as follows:
     Small localized networked (e.g. LAN) tend to use broadcast transmission, whereas networks located over wide geographical areas (e.g. WAN) use point-to-point transmission.
Internet

     A set of networks connected by routers that are configured to communicate among a variety of network transmission technologies; A network of networks

Packet Switches

     Forwards packets. We turn data into chunks of data called packets to allow it to move more efficiently; One packet could be an entire image, or many packets could make up one image, it all depends upon the size of your image. When you think of packet switches think of routers and switches.

Bandwidth

     The transmission rate of packets through a transmission technology.

Ethernet

     Ethernet is a networking standard that made local networking possible. Several other standards exist.

TCP/IP

     Trasmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol. Made internetworking possible. Internetworking is the communication between networks; 'the internet'.

Metrics

     Speed is measured in electronic units (bits/second=bps), where: K = 103, M = 106, G = 109, etc. (e.g. 8Mbps = 8,000,000 bps)

     Size is measured in bits, where: Ki = 210, Mi = 220, Gi = 230, etc. (e.g. 1GiB = 1,073,741,824 bits)

     Use lower-case b for bits, upper-case B for Bytes. Being where: 1Mib = 128KiB; 1Mib = 1024Kib.

Overview of Networking

[2]
Protocols

     A protocol is a set of guidelines for communication among network entities. A protocol defines two things:

            1. Format and order of messages sent and received among network entities.

            2. Actions taken on message transmission and receipt.

Network Structure

     Networks take on the form of the following structures:

     Network Edge: Consists of hosts and applications, also including clients and servers.
     Network Core: Consists of interconnected routers; a network of networks (network cores exist within the internet, they are not synonomous).
     Physical Media: Communication links

End Systems

     End systems ("hosts") are individual computers that intitiate requests or provide services (e.g. personal computers, cellphones, server computers, etc).

Client/Server Model

     Hosts and servers communicate with one another via intermediate 'always on' computers; Client host requests/receives service from a server that is 'always on' (e.g. If you go on your browser and open up your email, then: Your browser is the client and the email is the server; Your list of emails is provided by your communication as the client with the email client/server).

Peer-to-peer model (P2P)

     Minimal or no use of dedicated servers. Directly from a user machine to a user machine. Think of Skype or BitTorrent.

Circuit Switching and Packet Switching

[3]
How is data transferred through a network?

     Two main ways: Circuit switching and packet switching.

     Circuit switching: dedicated circuit per call (telephone network). All bandwidth is dedicated to two machines for a period of time. (TDM) --phased out
     Packet switching: Data sent through net in discrete packets on shared media. Network core focuses on forwarding all of these packets to the correct locations.

Packet Switched Network

      Primary functions: Packet transmission, packet interpretation, and packet fragmentation.

Packet Switched vs. Circuit Switched Networks

[4][5]
Multiplexing

     Multiplexing means to combine, or mix-together. De-multiplexing means to separate out again.
Statistical Multiplexing refers to the way in which packet-switched networks share network resources.

Time Division Multiplexing (TDM)

     Time Division Multiplexing is a technique where a short time sample of each channel is inserted into the multiplexed data stream.

Statistical Time Division Multiplexing (STDM)

     Statistical Time Division Multiplexing uses intelligent devices capable of identifying when a terminal is idle. They allocate time only to lines when required. This means that more lines can be connected to a transmission medium as this device statistically compensates for normal idle time in data communication lines.

Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM)

     A circuit-switched based network which allocates partitions of bandwidth in 'frequencies' to multiple users over time.

Four Sources of Packet Delay

     Nodal Delay: The accumulation of processing, queueing, transmission, and propagation delay. It is the time from data sent to data received.

        1. Processing Delay: The time taken to create and attach a header to packet(s) of data, created from destination data and protocol guidelines. Think of sitting at home charting on a map how you will get to the movie theatre you're wanting to watch a movie at. You need to recognize the laws of the streets you're taking, and consider how to get there the fastest. This map is the header and you (and whoever you're taking, if anyone) is the data/packet.

        2. Queueing Delay: The time taken waiting for packet to initiate transmission. Think of standing in line to get a movie ticket, you're waiting for your turn to even enter the movie theater, much less to get to the correct room or finish the movie.

        3. Transmission Delay: The time it takes to get packet(s) into the propagation medium. The propagation medium is whatever method the packet(s) use to get from client location to destination, that being something like: coaxial wire, fibre optic cables, satellite, etc. This would be you entering the room for the movie you're going to watch.

        4. Propagation Delay: The length of time taken for all packet(s) to reach the destination. This would be you finally finish the movie.

Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) vs User Datagram Protocol (UDP)

     TCP: Safe and reliable data transfer protocol. Requires a 3-way handshake; The first computer (instigator of connection) sends a 'SYN', the second computer (receiver of instigation) accepts/denies this SYN Provides and if accepting sends back a 'SYN ACK', finally the first computer upon receiving and accepting this SYN ACK sends back a 'ACK RECEIVED'. TCP has the following features:

         1. Reliable Data Transmission (Guaruntee of data delivery; if lost is resent)
         2. Flow Control
         3. Connection-oriented Service
         4. Congestion Control


UDP: Connection-less fast data-transfer protocol, no session established. Known as the 'fire and forget' protocol. UDP pretty much offers no features other than being fast.
